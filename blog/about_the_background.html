About the Background

If you have spent any time on this website, you have probably noticed the animated background — a slow, rotating web of glowing points fanning outward from a central origin. This is not just a decorative flourish. It is a visualization of the large-scale structure of the Universe, specifically inspired by how we actually map it.

What Is It Showing?

The background shows the Large-scale structure of the Universe as revealed by galaxy surveys. Each glowing point represents a galaxy, distributed around light cones that radiate outward from the center. The cones represent the survey footprints of real astronomical surveys, which map the 3D distribution of galaxies across the sky and through cosmic time.

In the Dark mode you may notice a bright glowing point at the center. It represents the Big Bang , which is the origin of all galaxies and structure in the Universe, including us. The colour of galaxies appears to have gradient from blue to red as the galaxies get farther away from the center. This is a visual representation of the cosmological redshift, whic is the stretching of light to longer wavelengths as the Universe expands.

If you ever switch to Light mode , the color scheme inverts. The central glow darkens, represent the Dark energy that makes up ~70% matter-energy content of the Universe and is driving its accelerated expansion. The galaxies become a gradient of dark amber, representing the Dark matter clumps, which make up ~25% of the Universe and provide the gravitational scaffolding for structure formation. The overall brightness represent for the Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB), which is the afterglow of the Big Bang and flies across the Universe. The CMB is the oldest light we can see, and it provides a snapshot of the Universe when it was just 380,000 years old — a baby picture of the cosmos that contains the seeds of all future structure. You may also noticed that there is a region on the left of the background is more opaque than the rest. This represent the part of the Universe that we can not observe. Since the speed of light is limited, we can only see a portion of the Universe called Observable Universe.

Why This?

I wanted something that reflected what I actually work on, which is cosmology, the study of the birth, evolution, ultimate fate, and the dynamic of the Universe.

It is also a reminder that the night sky is not just a backdrop. Every point of light is a galaxy containing hundreds of billions of stars, placed there by the same gravitational physics and initial conditions we are trying to constrain through surveys, simulations, and statistical inference.
